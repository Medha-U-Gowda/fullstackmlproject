🔬 Understanding Genetic Risk

Learn what SNP values, gene expression levels, and other markers mean for your genetic health assessment.

⚠️

Factors Affecting Genetic Risk

Genetic diseases stem from pathogenic mutations in DNA that can impair essential biological functions. Unlike a temporary infection, these risks are often systemic and lifelong, potentially leading to chronic disability. Several key factors influence your overall risk level.

🎂

Age

Many genetic conditions have variable expressivity — symptoms may only appear later in life as cellular damage accumulates, or during specific developmental stages.

⚧️

Gender

Certain risks are sex-linked (carried on X or Y chromosomes) or hormonally influenced, making some conditions more prevalent or severe in one gender.

👨‍👩‍👧

Family History

This reflects your hereditary load. A strong family history increases the probability that you carry specific risk alleles passed down through generations.

🧬

SNP Values

Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs) are individual "letter" variations in your DNA code. These values represent risk stratification — ranging from benign variants to critical pathogenic mutations.

📈

Gene Expression Values

While SNPs are your "instruction manual," gene expression measures how much of that instruction is actually being followed by the cell — known as transcriptional activity.

🧬

SNP Value Meanings (0 – 2 in dataset · 1–10 clinical scale)

Each SNP marker is scored on a scale. A higher score indicates a stronger association with disease risk. The bar beside each level shows relative severity.

Level Clinical Meaning Severity
1
Very common variant with negligible clinical impact.
2
Common polymorphism typically considered benign.
3
Low-impact variation occasionally associated with mild biological effects.
4
Variant of uncertain significance requiring additional evidence.
5
Moderate association with disease susceptibility.
6
Genetic variation linked to increased predisposition.
7
Strong risk marker with high probability of influencing phenotype.
8
High penetrance mutation often seen in hereditary disease cases.
9
Severe pathogenic mutation with strong disease correlation.
10
Critical mutation directly associated with major genetic disorders.
📈

Gene Expression Levels (1 – 5)

Gene expression measures how actively a gene is being transcribed into protein. Level 3 is normal; deviations in either direction can signal disease-related processes.

Level Clinical Meaning Activity
1
Gene is silenced or significantly downregulated.
2
Low expression producing limited protein activity.
3
Normal physiological expression level.
4
Upregulated expression indicating heightened cellular activity.
5
Extreme overexpression potentially associated with disease processes.

Obtaining Your Genetic Data

National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI)

Users can obtain genetic sequence information and SNP datasets from publicly available biomedical databases. NCBI hosts genomic datasets used by researchers worldwide and is one of the largest such resources available.
